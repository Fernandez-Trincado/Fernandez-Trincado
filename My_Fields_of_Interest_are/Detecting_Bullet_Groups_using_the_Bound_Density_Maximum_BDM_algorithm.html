Detecting Bullet Groups using the Bound Density Maximum (BDM) and Friend-of-Friend (FOF) algorithm

MultiDark Database

    We use a halo catalog from the Bolshoi Simulation detected with the Bound Density Maximun (BDM) algorithm. We select all halos with mass greater than 10^13 Msun for further analysis. Each halo in this sample has a quantity called Xoff that corresponds to the distance halo center (the minimum of the gravitational potential) to the center of mass normalized by the virial radius (Rvir). Values of Xoff equal to zero indicate relaxed halos, while Xoff>0 indicates the existence of perturbed halos which can be considered candidates to be Bullet Groups. In Figure 1 we compare two samples: halos with masses betwee10^130.25. For clusters we find that 0% in the sample have Xoff>0.25. In the right panel of Figure 1 we have the cumulative distribution of physical displacements (d). In this panel we see that a fraction 0.01% of the population has a displacement d > 200 kpc h^{-1}. For clusters this fractions increases to 0.1%.

    Figure 1: Left panel: Cumulative distribution of Xoff. The grey (red) line corresponds to groups (clusters) Right panel: Cumulative distribution of displacements for the same samples.

    Figure 2: Same as Figure 1, but this time the halos are split according to its circular velocity. Group halos are taken to have circular velocities between 350 km s^-1 to 1000 km s^-1 and Cluster halos are selected to have circular velocities larger than 1000km/s.

    Figure 3: Similar to Figure 2. In the right panel we overplot the cumulative distribution in terms of the redshift scaled (z=0), equaation 1 in Forero-Romero et al. (2010) that predicts the cumulative fraction of separations between gaseous and dark matter components in simulated clusters. The main conclusion is that the integrated probabilities are found in the same ballpark region defined by Forero-Romero et al. (2010).
